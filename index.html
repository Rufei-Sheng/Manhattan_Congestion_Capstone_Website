  • Methodology

    Nested Logit Models

    In order to quantify the impact of the Manhattan Congestion Pricing policy, we need to compare the simulated ridership under the new pricing scenario against the simulated baseline scenario. For that purpose, we will rst use the LEHD/LODES as a proxy for urban mobility, then train a model based on choices reported in ACS and actual proportions of taxi, FHV and shared FHV observed in the data, and nally compare the simulated ridership to prior observed preferences to assess the predicted modal shifts.

Project Background

Much of New York's growing population nds itself regularly frustrated while traversing the city. Subways are experiencing an increasing amount of delays due to an aging infrastructure. Driving speeds in Manhattan's Central Business District (south of 60th street) have slowed to an average of 7.2 mph, with speeds in the Midtown core" 30% slower than the rest of the CBD. While the subway and bus are still the third and fourth most popular ways to travel in NYC (after private vehicles and walking), public transit ridership has been declining since 2015, exacerbating nancial challenges at the MTA. Department of Transportation (DOT) ocials attribute the loss in ridership to consumers increasingly choosing For-Hire Vehicles (FHV), particularly during o ff-peak hours. The challenges facing NYC's complex transportation ecosystem have motivated this research.


New York lawmakers have responded to these challenges with an initiative unprecedented in the United States - congestion pricing. With aims to relieve congestion and recover some diverted revenue, New York State's Department of Taxation and Finance imposed a congestion surcharge on February 2, 2019 aimed at taxis and FHVs. The tax adds $2.75 to FHV trips, $2.50 to taxi trips and $.75 to ride-share or pool trips. The tax applies to every trip that begins, ends or passes through Manhattan south of 96th Street. On April 1, 2019 the NY State budget passed, approving a future congestion tax for all vehicles entering Manhattan's CBD below 60th St. Revenue from the tax are earmarked for MTA capital investments, but this larger scale congestion pricing scheme is not scheduled to be in e ect until December 2020. The tax will collect around $11 from both for-hire and private vehicles, and will include exemptions for low income New Yorkers and drivers with disabilities.

Motivation

Why we need to care about this new policy?

While transportations advocates eagerly anticipate the revenue from the congestion pricing plan, many drivers, particularly from the outer boroughs and suburban areas, are skeptical of the plan's potential to reduce trac congestion and lament paying a tax aimed at repairing a public transit system they may not use. Regardless of one's political opinion of the plan, the circumstances emphasize the importance of understanding the e ffect of pricing changes on people's transportation choices.

Goals

Open data are used to model the dynamics behind transportation mode shift in New York City. We use the data-driven model to explore the impacts of the initial Feb. 2, 2019 congestion tax and anticipate the mode shift that may be provoked by the December 2020 implementation of larger scale congestion pricing, as well as an alternative scenario in which MTA prices were increased by 30% in order to fund repairs.
